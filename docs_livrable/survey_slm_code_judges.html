Systematic Survey · State of the Art

Collaborative Small Language Models as Reliable Code Judges

From LLM-as-a-judge to efficient, sovereign Generator–Judge pipelines for code correctness

Author · Maryam Khallala Field · Software Engineering & LLMs Version · 1.0 — June 2026 Scope · 2023–2026
Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) generate code at near-human proficiency, yet their cost, privacy exposure, and propensity for plausible-but-incorrect output limit industrial adoption. A growing body of work reframes quality assurance as a judging problem: rather than scaling the generator, a separate model verifies candidate solutions. This survey synthesizes the state of the art at the intersection of three threads — (i) Small Language Models (SLMs) for code, (ii) the LLM-as-a-judge paradigm and its specialization to code correctness, and (iii) Generator–Judge reranking/selection pipelines. We organize the literature into a taxonomy, compare benchmarks (HumanEval, MBPP, CoderEval, MultiPL-E) and reliability metrics (pass@k, Cohen's κ, FDR), and analyze the efficiency/sovereignty arguments motivating local deployment. We identify a concrete research gap: the lack of a systematic evaluation of fine-tuned, collaborative decoder-only SLM judges, and outline a research agenda built on hard-negative training via mutation testing and team-based judging.

1Introduction & motivation

Code LLMs such as GPT-4 and the Llama / Code Llama family reach strong performance on basic programming tasks[1][2], but the software-engineering industry faces a scaling bottleneck: hosting a 70B-parameter model requires multi-GPU clusters costing on the order of tens of thousands of dollars, whereas an SLM runs on a single consumer GPU.[3] Two further problems compound the cost issue: LLMs frequently produce code that is syntactically valid but logically flawed (a manifestation of hallucination)[4], and most candidate-selection systems only rank outputs rather than delivering a strict binary correctness verdict suitable for automated-testing integration.[5]

In parallel, open SLMs — Qwen2.5-Coder, DeepSeek-Coder, Gemma, Code Llama — now deliver high specialized performance at a fraction of the parameter count[6][7][2], making them credible candidates for targeted verification tasks. This survey maps how these threads converge on a single idea: a specialized SLM judge, possibly deployed as a team, can approach the reliability of much larger models while remaining local, cost-efficient, and sustainable.[8]

2Scope & methodology

We conducted a semi-structured narrative survey, prioritizing peer-reviewed venues (ICSE, ASE, ICML, EMNLP, NeurIPS) and high-quality preprints from 2023–2026, with seminal earlier work retained for context. Inclusion criteria: relevance to (a) code generation/evaluation with language models, (b) the judge/evaluator paradigm, or (c) efficiency of small vs. large models. Three guiding research questions structure the review:

  • RQ1 — How capable are SLMs at code generation relative to LLMs, and under what budgets?
  • RQ2 — Can language models reliably judge code correctness, and how is that reliability measured?
  • RQ3 — Do collaborative / fine-tuned SLM judges close the gap with large models?

3Background & the Generator–Judge paradigm

The dominant route to higher-quality code is over-generation + selection: sample K candidates, then choose the best. Selection strategies range from execution-based filtering (unit tests, when available) to learned rerankers and, most recently, model-based judges. The judge paradigm originates in open-ended NLP evaluation — LLM-as-a-judge with MT-Bench showed strong agreement with human preferences[9] — and a 2025 survey, From Generation to Judgment, consolidates judging input/output formats (pointwise scoring, pairwise comparison, pass/fail classification) and methodologies.[10] A dedicated literature review now frames LLM-as-a-judge specifically for software engineering tasks.[11]

4Axis A — Small Language Models for code

Open code SLMs have advanced rapidly: Code Llama[2], DeepSeek-Coder[6], Qwen2.5-Coder[7] and Gemma for edge/mobile[12] deliver competitive results at 1–7B scale. Recent empirical studies systematically benchmark 20+ open SLMs (0.4–10B) on HumanEval, MBPP and efficiency-oriented suites, characterizing the accuracy/latency/cost frontier and confirming that the smallest viable model is often task-dependent.[13] Dedicated SLM surveys catalogue architectures, training, and on-device constraints.[14] A complementary line quantifies the energy footprint of small vs. large models for programming, grounding the sustainability argument.[15]

5Axis B — LLM/SLM-as-a-judge for code correctness

Generic preference judging does not transfer directly to code: correctness is objective and often hinges on subtle logic. CodeJudge evaluates code generation with LLMs without relying on test execution, eliciting structured correctness assessments.[16] MCTS-Judge applies test-time scaling (Monte-Carlo Tree Search) to strengthen LLM judgment of code correctness.[17] Yet studies also reveal systematic failure modes: models struggle to verify code against natural-language specifications, over-accepting plausible but wrong solutions.[18] On the small end, SLMJury provides one of the first systematic evaluations of whether SLMs can judge as well as large ones, reporting that careful prompting and aggregation narrow — but do not erase — the gap.[19]

Crucially, the closest work to the thesis — Crupi, Tufano et al., Improving Code Generation via Small Language Model-as-a-Judge — shows that off-the-shelf SLMs judge code poorly zero-shot, but after targeted fine-tuning their agreement with ground truth (Cohen's κ) rises substantially, matching or exceeding much larger judges and execution-free rerankers.[20]

6Axis C — Reranking, selection & training the judge

Coder-Reviewer reranking scores candidates by the likelihood the instruction is reconstructed from the code[21]; RankEF casts selection as multi-task learning over execution and non-execution signals, but optimizes ranking rather than binary classification reliability (e.g., False Discovery Rate).[5] Turning a generator-grade SLM into a discriminative judge typically combines (i) a classification head on the last hidden state, (ii) parameter-efficient fine-tuning via QLoRA[22], and (iii) supervision with hard negatives — buggy variants produced by semantic-preserving but logic-breaking mutation testing (operator, boundary, and control-flow mutations) seeded from correct HumanEval / CoderEval solutions.[23]

7Axis D — Collaborative / ensemble judging

The frontier idea is to replace a single judge with a team of specialized SLMs whose verdicts are aggregated, trading a modest compute increase for reliability competitive with 70B-class models while keeping inference local.[20][8] Open questions concern aggregation rules (majority, weighted, senior-review), judge diversity, and guarding against correlated errors and preference leakage when generators and judges share lineage.[10]

Why classification, not ranking

For automated-testing integration a system must answer "is this correct?" — a binary verdict with controlled False Discovery Rate — not merely "which candidate is best". Most rerankers optimize the latter, leaving classification reliability under-measured.[5]

10Research gap & agenda

Identified gap

Current literature lacks a systematic evaluation of modern decoder-only SLMs as autonomous, binary code judges, trained on hard negatives and operated collaboratively, with reliability reported as classification metrics (κ, FDR) on pragmatic benchmarks rather than ranking quality on toy tasks.[5][20]

This survey motivates a three-objective agenda, consistent with the thesis proposal:

  1. Benchmark SLM architectures (1B–7B) on binary code classification, reporting κ and FDR on HumanEval and CoderEval.[24]
  2. Specialized fine-tuning with mutation-generated hard negatives via QLoRA, to sharpen discrimination of near-invisible logical errors.[22][23]
  3. Collaborative team of SLM judges with principled aggregation, targeting reliability competitive with 70B-class models at ≈97% lower hardware cost and a markedly smaller carbon footprint.[8][15]

11Conclusion

The field is shifting from "scale the generator" to "verify with a specialized judge". SLMs make this shift economically and environmentally attractive, and early evidence shows that fine-tuned — and especially collaborative — SLM judges can rival far larger models on code-correctness decisions. The open problem is rigorous, classification-oriented evaluation of such teams on realistic benchmarks. A thesis combining hard-negative mutation training, QLoRA specialization, and team-based judging directly addresses this gap and aligns with the 2026 priorities of efficient, sovereign, and sustainable AI for software engineering.
