1. Executive Summary
RetinaSight AI is a multi-role, cloud-native clinical intelligence platform designed to support the detection, diagnosis, and staged grading of diabetic retinopathy (DR) and a broad spectrum of other retinal pathologies, including age-related macular degeneration (AMD), glaucomatous optic neuropathy, retinal vein occlusion (RVO), and macular oedema.
The platform addresses three distinct user journeys: (1) a Clinical Diagnosis module for ophthalmologists and general practitioners who require AI-assisted fundus image analysis and structured blood vessel morphology reporting; (2) a Simulation & Learning module for medical students and interns to build diagnostic competency through curated, expert-labelled case libraries, guided simulations, and knowledge evaluations; and (3) an Administration module for platform managers to curate datasets, configure evaluations, manage AI model lifecycles, and govern user access.
The system is intended to reduce diagnostic latency in underserved settings, standardise grading quality, and serve as a credentialled training environment aligned with international ophthalmology curricula.

2. Background & Problem Statement
2.1 Clinical Context
Diabetic retinopathy is the leading cause of preventable blindness worldwide, affecting an estimated 103 million adults (IDF 2025). Early-stage DR and other vision-threatening retinal diseases are frequently asymptomatic, making opportunistic fundus screening critical. Key barriers include:
Shortage of trained ophthalmologists, particularly in low- and middle-income countries
Inconsistent grading quality and inter-reader variability between clinicians
Delayed diagnoses due to manual image review backlogs in high-volume screening programmes
Insufficient clinical training infrastructure for the next generation of ophthalmologists

2.2 Opportunity
Deep learning models trained on large fundus datasets now achieve expert-level sensitivity and specificity for DR grading. RetinaSight AI operationalises these models within a purpose-built clinical workflow, augmenting—not replacing—physician decision-making, while simultaneously serving as a scalable educational environment.

3. Scope & Objectives
3.1 In Scope
AI-assisted DR grading (5-level ETDRS/ICDR scale) and lesion detection
Retinal blood vessel segmentation, calibre measurement, and morphology reporting
Detection of co-occurring pathologies: AMD, glaucoma, RVO, macular oedema
Curated annotated case dataset library for learning and simulation
Formative and summative knowledge evaluation engine
Administrative tooling: dataset curation, model management, user governance
DICOM and HL7 FHIR R4 interoperability

3.2 Out of Scope (v1.0)
Ultra-widefield or OCT image analysis (planned v2.0)
Direct prescribing or treatment recommendation engine
Real-time video fundoscopy streaming
Native mobile applications (iOS / Android)

4. Stakeholders & User Personas
4.1 Persona 1 — Dr. Amina (Ophthalmologist / General Practitioner)
Dr. Amina manages a busy diabetes clinic and screens 40–60 patients per week. She needs rapid, reliable AI-assisted grading with explainable outputs she can trust before countersigning a report. She is proficient with digital imaging tools but has limited tolerance for complex UX.
4.2 Persona 2 — Karim (4th-Year Medical Student / Intern)
Karim is preparing for his ophthalmology rotation. He has theoretical knowledge of DR but limited hands-on exposure to real fundus images. He needs a safe, low-stakes environment to practise grading and receive instant expert feedback. He primarily uses a laptop and occasionally a tablet.
4.3 Persona 3 — Fatima (Platform Administrator / Dataset Curator)
Fatima is a biomedical informaticist responsible for maintaining the quality and integrity of the image dataset, deploying updated AI models, configuring evaluation question banks, and ensuring regulatory compliance. She requires powerful administrative tools with audit transparency.

5. Functional Requirements
5.1 Module 1 — Clinical Diagnosis (Doctor Perspective)
IMAGE ANALYSIS & AI DIAGNOSTICS
The clinical module provides ophthalmologists and general practitioners with an end-to-end workflow: from secure image ingestion through AI-powered lesion detection, DR grading, vessel analysis, and structured report export.
Accept fundus images in JPEG, PNG, and DICOM 3.0 formats via drag-and-drop or PACS push
Run AI inference within 5 seconds to produce a 5-level DR grade with confidence score
Generate colour-coded heatmap overlays pinpointing microaneurysms, hard exudates, soft exudates, intraretinal haemorrhages, neovascularisation, and disc abnormalities
Perform automated vessel segmentation with artery/vein classification and calibre estimation (A:V ratio, fractal dimension, tortuosity index)
Screen for AMD (drusen, geographic atrophy, CNV), glaucomatous cupping (CDR), and RVO
Present a structured finding summary with severity classification aligned to ETDRS/ICDR grading standards
PATIENT & REPORT MANAGEMENT
Maintain a longitudinal patient record with image history, grading trends, and clinical notes
Side-by-side comparison viewer for follow-up assessment
Generate printable and exportable PDF reports with images, findings, and recommendations
HL7 FHIR R4 API for bidirectional EHR/EMR integration
Second-opinion workflow: annotate image and securely share with a remote colleague

5.2 Module 2 — Simulation & Learning (Student / Intern Perspective)
CASE LIBRARY & GUIDED LEARNING
The learning module exposes trainees to a rich, expert-annotated fundus image library, structured as case-based learning journeys. Cases are tagged by pathology, severity, image quality, and difficulty level.
Browse and filter case library by pathology type, ETDRS grade, patient demographics (anonymised), and difficulty
View clinical vignette, patient history, and examination findings before revealing the fundus image
Interactive annotation canvas: trainee draws suspected lesion locations before seeing expert overlay
Simulation mode: trainee submits a grade, then reveals step-by-step expert reasoning and annotated reference image
Annotated atlas with canonical reference images per pathology and per severity grade
KNOWLEDGE EVALUATION
Configurable assessment sessions: MCQ, image-based diagnosis, grading classification, and vessel analysis questions
Automated scoring with immediate feedback and explanation after each response
Summative examinations with timed sessions, score reporting, and pass/fail thresholds
Progress dashboard: completion rate, average score by category, improvement trend over time
Certificate of completion generation upon achieving configured competency thresholds

5.3 Module 3 — Administration
DATASET MANAGEMENT
The administration module provides full lifecycle management of the clinical image dataset underpinning both the AI model and the learning library.
Batch image ingestion pipeline with automatic metadata extraction (modality, acquisition date, device)
Multi-rater expert annotation tool with consensus adjudication workflow (majority vote or senior review)
Dataset versioning and changelog for reproducibility and model traceability
Quality control checks: resolution, illumination uniformity, focus scoring; flagging and quarantine of low-quality images
GDPR-compliant de-identification pipeline (face/identifier scrubbing)
EVALUATION & CONFIGURATION
Question bank editor: create, tag, and version MCQ and image-based questions
Assessment builder: configure question selection rules, timing, pass mark, and certificate template
AI model registry: upload new model checkpoints, compare benchmark metrics, promote to production, roll back
USER & PLATFORM GOVERNANCE
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC): Doctor, Student, Intern, Annotator, Reviewer, Admin, Super-Admin
Audit trail with immutable log of all data access, model inferences, annotations, and configuration changes
Platform KPI dashboard: active users, images processed, evaluation completion rates, AI model performance over time

6. Non-Functional Requirements
6.1 Performance
AI inference latency ≤ 5 seconds (95th percentile) under standard clinical server load
Web UI interaction response time ≤ 2 seconds (95th percentile) for all user interactions
Dataset ingestion pipeline capable of processing 10,000 images per batch without service degradation
6.2 Security & Compliance
TLS 1.3 for all data in transit; AES-256 encryption for all data at rest
Full GDPR (EU 2016/679) and HIPAA (45 CFR Part 164) compliance
Design and documentation aligned with FDA 510(k) SaMD pathway and EU MDR Annex I for the AI diagnostic module
OWASP Top 10 mitigations; annual penetration testing; SAST/DAST in CI pipeline
6.3 Availability & Scalability
System SLA: 99.5% monthly uptime (excluding planned maintenance windows)
Horizontal auto-scaling to support up to 10,000 concurrent users
RPO ≤ 1 hour; RTO ≤ 4 hours via automated backups and multi-AZ deployment
6.4 AI Model Quality
DR grading model: AUROC ≥ 0.95, sensitivity ≥ 0.90, specificity ≥ 0.90 on held-out validation set
Vessel segmentation: Dice coefficient ≥ 0.85 on DRIVE benchmark dataset
All AI outputs must include a calibrated confidence score and explainability heatmap (Grad-CAM or equivalent)

7. Proposed Technical Architecture & Stack
7.1 Architecture Overview
RetinaSight AI follows a microservices architecture deployed on Kubernetes, with dedicated service clusters for inference workloads (GPU-accelerated) and general application workloads (CPU). A unified API Gateway exposes controlled surfaces to each frontend application. Event-driven messaging (Kafka) decouples the ingestion pipeline from the inference and notification services.

7.3 AI / ML Pipeline Detail
The AI pipeline is implemented as an asynchronous microservice. Incoming images are validated, pre-processed (CLAHE normalisation, resize to 512×512), and passed to the inference service running on GPU nodes. The primary model is a ResNet-50 / EfficientNet-B4 ensemble fine-tuned on the EyePACS, MESSIDOR-2, and APTOS-2019 datasets. Vessel segmentation uses a U-Net variant trained on DRIVE and CHASEDB1. ONNX Runtime with TensorRT optimization provides sub-5-second inference on NVIDIA T4 GPUs.
Models are tracked and versioned via MLflow; champion/challenger deployment via Argo Rollouts canary strategy
Explainability: Grad-CAM++ heatmaps generated post-inference and stored alongside the image result
Model drift monitoring via Evidently AI; automatic alert when accuracy degrades beyond configurable threshold

9. Key Data Entities
The core data model comprises the following primary entities and their relationships:
Patient: anonymised demographic data, linked to one or more FundusStudy records
FundusStudy: acquisition metadata (date, device, laterality) + one or more FundusImage records
FundusImage: raw image file reference (MinIO), DICOM metadata, quality score
AIResult: DR grade, confidence, pathology flags, heatmap reference, model version, inference timestamp
VesselAnalysis: segmentation mask reference, A:V ratio, tortuosity index, fractal dimension
ClinicalReport: doctor-authored narrative, findings summary, AI result reference, export history
Case (Learning): linked FundusImage, expert annotation set, clinical vignette, difficulty tag, pathology tags
Assessment: configuration (question count, time limit, pass mark), linked to User attempt records
UserAttempt: question responses, score, timestamp, feedback snapshots
AnnotationSet: polygon/point coordinates, label, annotator, consensus status, reviewer sign-off
